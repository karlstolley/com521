Reading Questions

For 9/12

  • What four problems does Miller identify with the teaching of technical writing? Have you seen any of these problems in how you have been taught in the field?
  • Driskill argues that, “because of rapid changes in business environments and within companies, many rhetorical situations must be redefined to achieve greater congruence between organizational situations and rhetorical situations” (p. 67). How would Driskill’s argument have to change, based on the proliferation of digital communication technologies (as Driskill herself admits it would in her prefatory essay, pp. 55-56)?
  • Rude states in her prefatory essay that her article originated in her classroom, and has returned there in terms of how she teaches report writing. Imagine that you were teaching a technical writing course to undergraduates. How might you use her rhetorical decision-making method to build an assignment in that class? What would the assignment be? How would you introduce the idea of rhetorical inquiry, and prepare students for writing it?
  • Both Rude and Johnson are concerned with the idea of rhetorical inquiry, and of epistemology. How does Johnson’s notion of “audience involved” compare with the picture of rhetorical decision-making and inquiry that Rude sets up in her article?

For 8/29

  • Connors notes that, following World War II, “[s]chools seemed to ignore the changing conditions of the field, and when the journals began to print technical writing articles again after the war’s end, the articles had subtly changed focus” (p. 12). What was this subtle change in focus, and what does Connors identify as the consequences and impact for the field in that subtle change?
  • Rutter claims that “recent research on writing in the workplace and recent applications of anthropological concepts of culture to work place environments...was never meant to supplant humanistic values that place being and knowing before doing and writing” (p. 29). How do we locate Rutter’s claim amidst the theory/practice binary in our field? How does his claim help or hurt the discussion between academy and workplace?
  • In her article on gender and technology within tech comm, Durack argues that “men’s and women’s experiences of technology are quite different” (p. 39). Can you think of some examples that you see where this difference is acknowledged and acted upon? Do you see examples in consumer electronics, higher education, or your workplace? What role can (or should) technical communicators play in dealing with gender differences vis-a-vis technology?
