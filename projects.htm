Projects

  1. Ongoing Projects

    • Professional Discussion: Choosing either a listserv (e.g., ATTW-L, CPTSC) to follow or following academics in technical communication and related fields on Twitter; posting and participation is encouraged, but not required.

    • Classroom Discussion: Actively engaging in weekly discussion, responding to peer work and presentations (30pts)

  3. Major Projects

    • Annotated Bibliography: Covers the past 5-7 years of a specific topic in technical communication or related fields, one that should be leading towards a possible dissertation topic or MS project; 20 key sources (articles, chapters, and books) with paragraph-long annotations. (draft due 10/3, final due 10/24; 25pts)

    • Article-Length Literature Review/Bibliographic Essay: Approaching publishable length & quality and based on the Annotated Bibliography; drafted with a specific technical communication journal in mind and according to that journal's submission guidelines and citation/formatting style. Should frame a particular argument for placing and understanding the last 5-7 years of research, and likely suggests or forecasts future research directions in the topic area. (draft due 11/21, final due 12/5; 25pts)

      Requirements

      • Length: 5000-6000 words (about 20-25 double-spaced pages)
      • Manuscript and Citation Format: Follow the guidelines for a specific journal that you think would publish your essay.

      The challenge to writing a good bibliographic essay or literature review is to avoid a big dumping of notes. A successful bibliographic essay will feature:

      • An argument as to how to understand or frame the work/theme/topic area that the bibliographic essay covers.
      • An appropriate organizational scheme to cover the work: chronology is rarely the best choice, unless your argument has to do with something that unfolds over time (e.g., how technical communication scholarship changed in the years after 9/11/2001). Arranging your essay around particular themes, the work of particular people (if there are only a few major figures), or particular sub-topics in the area are all plausible; however, your research topic will be the best thing to consider as you develop some form of organization. Think in terms of headings and outlines, if that will help you.
      • Judicious quotation from the sources that you cite. Summarize when possible, quote when necessary is one way to think about when to quote and when not.
      • Suggestions and directions for future research based on the argument that your bibliographic essay lays out (hint: thinking about suggestions/directions early on may very well help you to invent the argument that you advance in your essay).
